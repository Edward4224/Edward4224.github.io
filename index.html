Our investigation must therefore begin with the analysis of a commodity. A commodity is, in the first place, an object outside us, a thing that by its properties satisfies human wants of some sort or another. The nature of such wants, whether, for example, they spring from the stomach or from fancy, makes no difference. Nor are the nature and the amount of labor necessarily fixed by nature, which must be bestowed upon them to give them specific utility. But if we leave out of consideration the use value of commodities, they have only one property left: they are products of labor. In this capacity as products of labor, they are also values‚Äîcommodity values. It is only by being exchanged that the products of labor acquire as values a material existence separable from their corporeal existence as mere use-values. We may twist and turn a single commodity as we wish; it remains impossible to grasp it as a thing possessing value. As soon as a thing is useful, it is a use value. But this usefulness does not dangle in mid-air. It is conditioned by the physical properties of the commodity and has no existence apart from that commodity. A commodity, such as iron, corn, or a diamond, is therefore, so far as it is a material thing, a use value, something useful. This property of a commodity is independent of the amount of labor required to appropriate its useful qualities. When, however, we come to inquire into the commodity as an object of value, its analysis presents itself quite differently. The use of a commodity is determined by its properties, but its value is determined by the amount of labor embodied in it. A commodity, such as iron, corn, or a diamond, must therefore possess a value-form as well as a physical form.
